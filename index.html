A Mathematical Approach

A Ring of Cells

In order to illustrate the general principle of morphogenesis, I will first consider - closely following Prof. A. M. Turing's 1952 article - a particular configuration of cells: a ring of similar cells. Despite not modelling a real biological example, this configuration allows for a simple mathematical analysis and the associated theory can later be extended to more complex and practically useful cases.

Only two morphogens $X$ and $Y$ will be considered. Each cell exchanges morphogens by diffusion to the adje

\[ \begin{cases} \dfrac{dX_r}{dt} = f(X_r, Y_r) + \mu (X_{r + 1} - 2X_r + X_{r-1}) \\[1em] \dfrac{dY_r}{dt} = g(X_r, Y_r) + \nu (Y_{r + 1} - 2Y_r + Y_{r-1}) \end{cases} \qquad r = 1, ..., N \, . \]
  • $N \in \mathbb{N} \;$ number of cells in the ring;
  • $X_r, Y_r \in \mathbb{R}^+ \;$ concentration of the morphogens $X$ and $Y$ in cell $r$;
  • $f \, : \, \mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ rate of $X$ increase;

Some considerations can be made:

  • An isolated cell has equilibrium $X = h \, , \; Y = k$ when $f(h,k) = 0 = g(h, k)$.
  • The system has an equilibrium when $X_r = h \, , \; Y_r = k \; \forall r = 1, ..., N \,$.
